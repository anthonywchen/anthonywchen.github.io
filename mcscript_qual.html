Qualification Task: Judge if two answers are the same (MCScript)

Read the instructions and examples, even if you have done similar tasks, as the examples may have changed.


Then answer the 10 questions, referring to the examples when necessary. You will need at least 7 correct to pass. You will be paid whether you pass or not.


If you pass, you will receive the "mcscript" qualification. The qualification will allow you to work on the 1,000 HITs for this task that we will release over time.


In this task, you are given a passage, a question, a correct answer, and a predicted answer.

The task is to score how closely a predicted answer captures the same information (i.e. have the same meaning) as a correct answer using the passage, question, and human commonsense knowledge.

Scoring is on a scale from 1 to 5 .
A 1 score means the predicted answer captures none of the information of the correct answer, and thus is a completely wrong answer.
A 5 score means the predicted answer captures the same information as the correct answer, and thus is a completely right answer.


Note: A predicted answer may answer the question correctly, but to be given a score of 5, it must be equivalent to the correct answer.

Note: The score you give should not depend on the capitalization of the correct and predicted answer.

Note: A helpful tip for searching for the correct answer in the passage is using Control+F to search for it.

(Completely Right) Predicted answers that should recieve a 5:

  Some ways a predicted answer can get a score of 5.
  • It is clear that that the predicted and correct answers refer to the same person, place, or object.
  • The predicted and correct answers are synonyms.
 
Passage: I noticed my alarm clock had stopped working. I removed the old batteries by lifting them up, then I placed the new batteries in the same position.
Question: Who changed the batteries in the clock?
Correct Answer: The speaker
Predicted Answer: the narrator
Justification: The speaker refers to the same person as the narrator. More generally, if the correct answer and the predicted answer both refer to the same entity, then the predicted answer is correct, even if it is written differently.

Passage: …I heated up a frying pan with a little butter in it. Once the pan was hot, I poured my eggs in it…
Question: What were the eggs cooked in?
Correct Answer: The eggs were cooked in a pan.
Predicted Answer: a frying pan
Justification: As stated in the passage, the pan is indeed a frying pan. Therefore, the predicted answer matches the correct answer. The correct answer also has the phrase "the eggs were cooked in", however, this part is stated in the question and is not necessary to have in the predicted answer.

Passage: We chose not to make any new food, because we could still eat the leftover food. We took put it into a pan, and put that pan on the stove…
Question: Were they heating leftovers?
Correct Answer: Yes they were reheating leftovers.
Predicted Answer: yes
Justification: The predicted answer of "yes" is sufficient to answer the question and match the predicted answer.

Passage: We went to the mall and decided to split up and meet up two hours later. After we met back up, we ate some ice cream before we left…
Question: How long did they spend there?
Correct Answer: Three hours, because they shared ice cream after shopping
Predicted Answer: a few hours
Justification: We say that "a few hours" is similar enough to "three hours" since "few" generally includes "3". The predicted answer does not need to include "because they shared ice cream after shopping" because the question did not ask for a reason.

Passage: I was getting ready to set the alarm when I realized that's the batteries were dead and needed to be changed…
Question: when did they change the battery?
Correct Answer: When the clock did not work.
Predicted Answer: When they realized the batteries were dead
Justification: The clock not working is essentially the same event as the batteries dying. We therefore say this predicted answer is completely correct.

(Mostly Right) Predicted answers that should recieve a 4:

  A score of 4 means a predicted answer is perfect with a minor detail wrong. Some ways a predicted answer can get a score of 4.
  • The predicted answer is a little ungrammatical.
  • The predicted answer is a little unspecific (e.g. the correct answer is the city but the predicted answer is the country of the city).
  • The predicted answer gets the gender wrong (e.g. he vs she, him vs her).
  • The predicted answer gets the plurality wrong (e.g. he vs they, policeman vs policemen).
 
Passage: I went to the train station early, so I picked a spot on a bench and sat down. After a short while, everybody lined up to get on the train…
Question: How long did they wait at the station?
Correct Answer: a short while
Predicted Answer: a few minutes
Justification: A few minutes is a near perfect answer. However, the predicted answer says a "short while", which is broad enough that it could be interpreted as anytime from a few minutes to 30 minutes. We therefore give this predicted answer a 4 for not completely matching the correct answer.

Passage: We wanted to make sure the bathroom was clean. We got out glass cleaner, toilet bowl cleaner, paper towels, and a scrubber…
Question: What cleaning products did they use?
Correct Answer: Glass and toilet bowl cleaners.
Predicted Answer: glass cleaner , toilet cleaner , toilet cleaner
Justification: The predicted answer lists all the items in the correct answer, however, it repeats the "toilet cleaner" item. Because the predicted answer is correct but is a little repetitive, we give it a 4.

(Half Right) Predicted answers that should recieve a 3:

  Some ways a predicted answer can get a score of 3.
  • The prediction gets the details right, but is quite unspecific (see example 2)
  • The prediction gets half of the details right (see example 3).
  • The correct answer is a list but the prediction only gets some of the elements of the list.
  • The correct answer is a single item (person, object, etc) but the prediction adds some extra items.
  • The predicted answer gets the first name right but the last name wrong (or vice versa).
 
Passage: I put some very small tinder as well as some balled up pieces of newspaper. I lit the newspaper and added small pieces of wood to it…
Question: What did they use for fuel?
Correct Answer: Wood
Predicted Answer: Newspaper and wood
Justification: In cases where the correct answer is a noun or a list of nouns and the predicted answer either adds extra items or misses some of them, we generally give it a 3.

Passage: Taking a trip to celebrate a holiday is a wonderful experience. I decided to take a trip to New York City this year to celebrate New Years Eve…
Question: What event are they looking forward to most?
Correct Answer: New Year's Eve.
Predicted Answer: celebrating an annual holiday
Justification: While New Year's Eve is an annual holiday, it is too vague, as there are many holidays. We therefore give it a 3

Passage: I wanted to spruce up my house. I had three paintings of plumeria flowers waiting to be hung on the wall…
Question: what kind of art is it?
Correct Answer: Paintings of flowers
Predicted Answer: plumeria flowers
Justification: The predicted answer gets the fact that art is of flowers correct. However, it doesn't describe the kind of art (painting). We therefore consider this answer half correct.

Passage: I was reading when the light went out. It looked like that light bulb had burned out. I went to the hall closet and found a package of light bulbs…
Question: Where did they get the light bulb?
Correct Answer: From the hall closet
Predicted Answer: the living closet
Justification: The predicted answer gets the fact that location is a closet. However, the closet type is wrong. A living closet (whatever that is) is not the same as a hall closet. Therefore, the prediction is half correct.

(Mostly Wrong) Predicted answers that should recieve a 2:

  A score of 2 means that the predicted answer is incorrect, but there is some slight redeemable quality to it.
  • The predicted is very unspecific (see example 1).
  • The prediction gets some detail correct, but more than one thing wrong and/or is highly ungrammatical.
  • The predicted gets a detail wrong that significantly affects the meaning, but still gets some minor details correct.
 
Passage: I needed to clean up my flat. I had to get my broom and vacuum. I used the vacuum to clean up the floors that had rugs and carpets…
Question: Where did they vacuum?
Correct Answer: Rooms with rugs and carpet
Predicted Answer: in the flat
Justification: While the rooms with rugs and carpet are technically in the flat, the predicted answer needs to be much more specific and say which rooms.

Passage: My family decide to go to the beach. We packed some snacks. We packed a cooler with all our drinks and water…
Question: How did they keep lunch cold?
Correct Answer: probably with a cooler
Predicted Answer: yes , they put a cooler
Justification: The predicted answer includes the term "cooler" which is good. However, the word "yes" is unnecessary and "they put a cooler" is ungrammatical and doesn't make much sense.

Passage: I like to stay in touch with family and friends. We stay in touch by texting but we like to hear each others voices so we often call…
Question: Why does that person like to call?
Correct Answer: To catch up with family and friends
Predicted Answer: it was to talk to talk to talk to talk to talk to talk
Justification: The predicted answer not only rambles, but it misses the important detail of talking to family and friends. However, "to talk" is the same as "to catch up" in this context, so we will say the predicted is not completely wrong and give it a 2.

(Completely Wrong) Predicted answers that should recieve a 1:

  There are a number of ways a predicted answer can get a score of 1.
  • TRICKY!!! A predicted answer can technically be a correct answer to the question, but if it does not have the same meaning as the correct answer, it is a score of 1 for this task. (see example 1)
  • The predicted doesn't make any sense and/or is very ungrammatical.
  • The answer gets all details wrong (e..g the name(s) are completely wrong, places are completely wrong, etc).
  • The predicted answer is opposite of the correct answer (e.g. yes vs no, hot vs cold, etc.)
 
Passage: It was a cool spring evening and we wanted to have a family get together on the beach around a bonfire. … We lit the fire and began to cook so everything would be ready for our guests once they arrived.
Question: Why did they have a bonfire?
Correct Answer: to have a family gathering
Predicted Answer: to cook everything
Justification: While "to cook everything" is technically a correct answer to the question, for a predicted answer to be "right" in this task, it must also be EQUIVALENT (have the same meaning) to the correct answer. "to cook everything" is a completely different from "to have a family gathering". We therefore score the predicted answer a 1.

Passage: It was a cool spring evening and we wanted to have a family get together on the beach around a bonfire…
Question: Why did they have a bonfire?
Correct Answer: to have a family gathering
Predicted Answer: to have a family
Justification: The predicted answer has a lot of words in common with the the correct answer. However, it is more important to focus on the meaning. "Having a family", is completely different than "having a family gathering", so this gets a 1.

Passage: Tom needed to go to work. Unfortunately, his car would not start. He then looked online to find a taxi…
Question: When did they get on the taxi ?
Correct Answer: Morning
Predicted Answer: in the destination minutes
Justification: The predicted answer doesn't make sense and isn't grammatical.

Passage: We were excited about the awesome new mall and planned all week to visit it this past weekend!…
Question: When did they go to the shopping centre?
Correct Answer: last weekend
Predicted Answer: this weekend
Justification: Last weekend and this weekend have different meanings.

Passage: My wife and I flew on an airplane a couple of months ago . We were going to Las Vegas because a friend of mine was getting married…
Question: Why are they going to the destination?
Correct Answer: friends wedding
Predicted Answer: to married
Justification: "to married" is ungrammatical. Also, it seems like the predicted answer is implying the narrator is getting married, which is wrong.
